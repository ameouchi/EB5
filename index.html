EB-5 Capital Gerrymandering of Targeted Employment Areas
By Wuyahuang Li, Alberto Meouchi, Olivia Serra
12/09/2020

In her book Extrastatecraft, American urbanist Keller Easterling used this new-coined term to describe undisclosed political and financial activities in partnership with statecraft. In this article, the team uses Extrastatecraft as a conceptual framework, to investigate EB-5 capital gerrymandering of targeted employment areas across US cities. We use the data from the American Community Survey to analyze the differences in unemployment rates before and after the so-called EB-5 projects and proposed a way of evaluating the funding model in the conclusion section.

What is EB-5?

There is Such Thing As Free Lunch!



In 1990, the U.S. Congress created the fifth employment-based (i.e., EB-5) preference immigrant visa for foreign nationals eligible to become lawful permanent residents (or green card holders) by financing a “new commercial enterprise“ that will employ at least 10 American workers. For each EB-5 visa, one must invest at least $500,000 ($900,000 since 2019) through regional centers.



Illustration by Isabel Espanol

The regional center is a federally approved agent, often a private third-party, taking a commission from developers to connect them with foreign investors. Researchers at the Center for Real Estate Finance Research at New York University found that 99% of the immigrant investor involved in the EB-5 program invest in the targeted employment area (i.e., TEA). The EB-5 program did not gain popularity until the 2008 housing bubble drained conventional sources of capital. The EB-5’s purpose is not only to stimulate the U.S. economy through the creation and capital investment but to raise funding to invest in high unemployment areas. However, the US government decided to turn a blind eye on the qualification of regional centers and their standards on qualifying TEA as a way for the real estate market to recover from the financial crisis.





EB-5 Capital Gerrymandering and Consequences



Costing a total of $25 billion, Hudson Yards is the most expensive real-estate development project in US history. The primary developer of the 26-acre West Side Yards in midtown Manhattan is Related Companies – one of the largest developers in the US –and Oxford Property Groups. In addition to the investment capital of these developers, Hudson Yard collects $1.6 billion from the fifth employment-based (EB-5) visa program, the Golden Visa providing a fast track to trade US residency and citizenship with wealthy foreign investors for investment capital. In January 2017, the CityLab published a document revealing the joint census tracts to qualify Hudson Yard as a Targeted Employment Area. The map included in the document illustrates a highly selective manipulated boundary to qualify Hudson Yards as a TEA.



Public Advocate Jumaane Williams said Hudson Yards was cynically gerrymandered into a zone for “distressed areas,” linking it to public-housing in Harlem so that the new mega-development could quality for a visa program benefiting wealthy foreigners. (Photo by Tequila Minsky)

As a result, the unemployment rate is boosted from 5.0% to 12.6%, almost three times the national unemployment rate (4.4%) in the same year. The extremely odd route consists of 12 census tracts, five of which locate in Harlem 70 blocks away from the EB-5 funded projects in Hudson Yards. To make the Hudson Yards’ gerrymandering a more appealing case for USCIS for EB-5 funding, Empire State Development assists The Related Companies to include Central Park, a blank in the census database. The players committing such forms of gerrymandering range from private developers to state agents, taking up undisclosed economic and financial activities in partnership with the sanctioned authorities of the state.

How it Works

Financial Gerrymandering in New York City

At its core, EB-5 is a job-incentive policy yet fails to translate the employment figure into its target geography. Multiple state agencies are involved in the lawful yet unethical act of EB-5 capital gerrymandering, which further registers Hudson Yards as an extractstatecraft, and Harlem as a free lunch for everyone, every full-human as synonymous with white, western man. In the neoliberal context, social risks such as poverty and unemployment fall into the domain of self- care problems, and the state intervenes in redirecting capital in a reverse robinhood fashion and withdraws when confronting the pressure of real estate and capital.

Conclusions

What is next?

In 2019, the Department of Homeland Security published an EB-5 amendment, intending to regulate the regional centers and limit the gerrymandering practices of TEA. Although it was supposed to come into effect on November 21, 2019, President Trump signed a law extending the Regional Center Program through December 11, 2020. The future of EB-5 is still uncertain, but its potential effect to distort and reconfigure the geography of economics and racial justice still exists at a national level. Based on our case studies, we realize that some TEAs have witnessed an unemployment rate decrease after an adjacent EB-5 project was completed. More cases show otherwise. Therefore, we would like to propose to evaluate regional centers based on their ability to mitigate the unemployment reality, instead of the EB-5 capital they can allocate into the market.









The repairment of EB-5 is overdue. How else can it be fixed?
